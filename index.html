ENS Names (.eth)
An ENS name in your wallet looks like an NFT, but the NFT itself is basically a receipt. The "working version" of the name lives on the ENS contracts — and the records that say "send funds to this address" are stored separately.
Why you can't just use LIFEBOAT to move it
There are three things attached to your name that all need to move:

1. Owner — the wallet holding the NFT (controls who can transfer)
2. Manager — the wallet that can change the records
3. ETH Address — where funds sent to yourname.eth actually arrive

If you transfer just the NFT (the way LIFEBOAT moves a normal NFT), only the Owner moves. The Manager and ETH Address stay pointed at the compromised wallet. Anyone sending crypto to your name keeps sending it into the dead wallet. Do the ENS Send flow instead. It handles all three in the right order, in one signing session.
The fund-and-fire method (step by step)
This is the exact method used to rescue Kane's own ENS name. It works. The compromised wallet has no ETH for gas — that's the whole problem. So the play is: get the ENS app loaded and ready, figure out exact gas, send gas privately through the Quiet Fund tab, then immediately sign before the bot reacts.

1. Open app.ens.domains in a new tab. Connect MetaMask using the compromised wallet. Find your name. Click the Ownership tab, then Send. Type your clean wallet address.

2. Let the transaction try to fire. The wallet has no ETH, so it'll fail or show "insufficient funds." That's what you want. Note the gas amount MetaMask shows. The Send flow may have multiple transactions back-to-back (ETH record, Manager, Owner) — pad generously.

3. — select Ethereum, paste your funding wallet key, paste the compromised wallet address, enter 15–20% more than the gas estimate, and click Send Quiet Fund.

4. Wait about 10 seconds (count "10 Mississippi"). The funding transaction lands privately via MEV Blocker — the bot doesn't see it until it's confirmed.

5. Switch back to your ENS tab immediately and try the Send again. MetaMask will let you sign now. Sign the first transaction. Then the second. Then the third. Don't pause between them. The bot will eventually figure out there's gas in the wallet and try to drain — your transactions need to land first.

ENS sequences these in the correct order automatically: ETH record first (so funds stop flowing to the dead wallet immediately), Manager second, Owner last.

6. After all three confirm, switch to the Native Sweep tab and sweep any leftover ETH back to your clean wallet.

⚠ ENS names ONLY exist on Ethereum mainnet. Not Polygon, not Base, not any L2.
Exchange-Locked & Staked Tokens
Same playbook as ENS with one critical difference: you have to unlock the asset before the bot can be beaten.
DeFi stakes (Lido, Aave, Compound, NFT staking)
The unstake transaction has to happen FROM the compromised wallet, which means that wallet needs gas. Same choreography: open the unstake page, get it ready to sign, use Quiet Fund to send gas privately, then immediately sign the unstake. Once the assets are back in the wallet, rescue them with LIFEBOAT's normal flow.

Watch for cooldown periods. Lido is roughly 1–5 days, some others longer. If yours has a cooldown, you'll need to fund the wallet twice — once to initiate the unstake, then again days later to claim and transfer. The bot is patient. Plan accordingly.
Centralized exchanges (Coinbase, Binance, Kraken, etc.)
This is actually different and often easier — the assets aren't on your compromised wallet at all. They're in your exchange account, controlled by your exchange login (email + password + 2FA), not your seed phrase.

If your exchange account is still secure (only your wallet was compromised, not your email/2FA), log into the exchange and withdraw directly to your clean wallet address. No LIFEBOAT needed. Done.

⚠ If your email or 2FA was also compromised: contact the exchange's support immediately — most have account recovery and freeze procedures. Do NOT send anything to the compromised wallet. The exchange flow is your safest path.
Vested / locked tokens
You can't move what isn't unlocked yet. When the next vest unlocks, be ready with the same fund-and-fire choreography — the moment unlocked tokens hit the wallet, sweep them with LIFEBOAT before the bot can react.

Understanding What Happened
Q: How did I get hacked? I'm usually careful.
Being careful doesn't protect against everything. The most common vectors:

Malicious signature — You signed something that looked like a normal NFT listing, bid approval, or mint. It wasn't. It was a "permit" or "setApprovalForAll" that handed over control of your wallet. These are designed to be indistinguishable from real prompts.

Compromised seed phrase — Your seed phrase was stored somewhere digital — a screenshot, Notes app, email draft, Google Drive. It got found. Once someone has your seed, every wallet it generates on every chain is permanently theirs.

Malicious browser extension — A fake or compromised extension read your clipboard or intercepted your wallet data. Extensions have much more browser access than most people realize.

Phishing site — A site that looked like OpenSea, Blur, or another platform you trusted. Same logo, similar URL, different wallet drainer running underneath.

None of this means you were stupid. These attacks are specifically engineered to fool people who are actively engaged in NFTs — because active collectors click things and sign things. You got hit. Now we get your stuff out.
Q: Why didn't they take my NFTs immediately if they had access?
Because most drainer bots are built for one thing: instant ETH. Moving NFTs requires knowing which contracts to call, paying gas, handling ERC-721 vs ERC-1155 differences, and waiting for blocks. It's more complicated than just sweeping ETH.

The bot that hit you is probably sitting on your wallet right now watching for ETH to arrive so it can sweep it. That's its whole job.

This is your window. Their laziness is why your NFTs are still there. We exploit that window by moving everything privately before the bot can react.
Using This Tool
Q: What is MEV Blocker and why does it matter?
MEV Blocker is a private Ethereum RPC endpoint. Instead of broadcasting your transaction to the public mempool — where every bot in the world can see it waiting — it routes your transaction directly to validators in private.

The mempool is the trap. Normally when you send a transaction, it sits in a public waiting room for a few seconds. The drainer bot is watching that waiting room 24/7. The instant it sees ETH land in your compromised wallet, it submits its own drain transaction with higher gas to cut in line.

With MEV Blocker, your transactions are invisible until they're already in a block. The bot has zero reaction time because it never knew you were coming. That's going in through the garage.

After the Rescue
Q: Is the compromised wallet safe to use again?
No. Never use it again. It's community property now — the opposite of a wallet.

The seed phrase is permanently burned. Anyone who has it owns every wallet it can ever generate, on every chain, forever. The rescue moves your assets out — it doesn't undo the compromise. Write off that wallet and never send anything to it again.
Q: How do I make sure this doesn't happen again?
Seed phrase: Paper only. Not a photo. Not your Notes app. Not email. Not iCloud. A piece of paper in a safe physical place.

Hardware wallet: A Ledger or Trezor keeps your private key on a device that never touches the internet — even when you sign transactions.

Signing habits: If a site asks you to sign something and you're not 100% sure what it is — don't. Close the tab. Navigate directly to the official URL.

Two wallets: A "hot" wallet for active stuff with small amounts, and a cold storage wallet for anything you'd actually be upset to lose.
